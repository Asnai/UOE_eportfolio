Collaborative Discussion 2 Summary

For the past two decades, the commercial use of personal data has grown in wild-west fashion. Data trails were providing the foundation to some of the world’s largest companies. But now those days are coming to an end. Rahnama & Pentland (2022).

With the introduction of legal frameworks like GDPR, companies are being forced to adhere to data privacy laws like never before. They are being rushed to implement the laws as not adhering to these laws can lead to huge consequences. In the case of GDPR, some infringements can be subjected to fines up to 10 000 000 EUR, or in the case of an undertaking, up to 2 % of the total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year, whichever is higher. The European Commission (2016).

During the discussion Joy stated that General Data Protection Regulations (GDPR) offered a foundational framework for data protection laws, and I agree with this. GDPR has attained a worldwide norm and other countries are using it to model their data protection laws after it.

While countries try to base their privacy laws according to GDPR, there are always differences due to different law requirements in each country. One example of this is Thailand’s PDPA (Personal Data Protection Act) which was modeled after GDPR but doesn't apply to operations of public authorities. (PDPA, 2019). While these differences may look minor, for a company to implement this, it can be very tricky which is why I believe that there should be a universal data protection law to make it easier for companies to comply and everyone is judged on the same law. Alex, in his post, seemed to agree with me on this.

Joy partially agreed that there should be a universal framework, but it should apply to international transfer of data, and each nation should protect their citizen’s data with their own laws. Joy gave an example of how GDPR has achieved this across European Union member nations.
